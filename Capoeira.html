the Brazilian deadly dance

 
 

Capoeira is a Brazilian martial art that combines elements of dance, music and acrobatics, developed by enslaved Africans in Brazil during the colonial period, mainly in the 16th to 19th centuries. Originating in slave quarters and quilombos, where enslaved people took refuge, capoeira was a form of resistance and cultural preservation. Practitioners disguised their fighting movements as dancing to avoid repression from planters. In the 19th century, after the abolition of slavery in 1888, capoeira continued to be associated with criminals and was banned, with severe penalties for practitioners. At the beginning of the 20th century, masters such as Mestre Bimba and Mestre Pastinha were fundamental in the legitimization and systematization of capoeira, creating academies and promoting it as a form of culture and physical education. Mestre Bimba developed regional capoeira, while Mestre Pastinha focused on the more traditional capoeira Angola. Capoeira was officially recognized as a Brazilian cultural heritage and, in 2014, it was inscribed on the list of Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity by UNESCO. Today, capoeira is practiced worldwide, celebrated for its rich history and unique combination of art and fighting.
